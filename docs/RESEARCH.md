# Research notes

How to Read is a personal response-formatting preference.
It is not a medical treatment or a diagnostic tool.

Its defaults combine Morris Hannessen's reading preferences with established
plain-language and cognitive-accessibility guidance:

- Put the main point first.
- Prefer common words and short sentences.
- Use active voice when it makes the meaning clearer.
- Split dense text into short, meaningful blocks.
- Use headings, lists, whitespace, and relevant visuals as navigation aids.
- Reduce unnecessary syntactic complexity.

The W3C cognitive accessibility guidance recommends easy words, short sentences,
short blocks, unambiguous content, clear images, and helpful whitespace.
Its WCAG technique on sentence clarity also recommends simple sentence forms
and active voice.

The British Dyslexia Association style guide similarly emphasizes readable
language, structure, spacing, and presentation.

A controlled study by Wiseheart and colleagues found that adults with dyslexia
were more affected by syntactic complexity when working-memory demands were
higher. This supports reducing avoidable sentence complexity, but it does not
prove that one writing format works for everyone.

The skill intentionally does not prescribe a special "dyslexia font."
Studies of Dyslexie and OpenDyslexic did not find a general reading-performance
advantage. Individual comfort can still differ.
